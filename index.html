The Participation-Power Gap: What We Don't Know

  • Contentious politics tradition (Tarrow & Tilly; DOCA): relies on protest event counts; lacks a view into internal organizational structure
  • Civic engagement tradition (Verba, Schlozman, Brady): individual surveys
  • Unobserved: how respondents are relationally connected, longitudinal participation, and internal org structure
  • An understanding of the transmission mechanism between inputs (participation) and outputs (influence) remains ripe for exploration

POLIS tracks the organizational infrastructure between civic participation and political power.

What POLIS makes visible:

  • Who participates and how engagement deepens or atrophies over time
  • Internal structure: leadership density, relational architecture, recruitment networks

Outcomes (traced through multiple methods):

  • Policy wins and electoral influence via prospective case studies, interviews, organizational records
  • Relationships with officials via interviews with leaders, volunteers, staff, policymakers

NC

In North Carolina, purple line leaders targeted strategic races that shaped state power.

"Last year, the Carolina Federation made a strategic choice to focus our time and efforts on the Supreme Court race... We knocked over 300,000 doors, made over 600,000 phone calls, sent over 1.4 million text messages, and registered over 10,000 new voters."

Allison Riggs won her Supreme Court seat by 734 votes out of 5.5 million cast—the closest statewide race in NC history.

Durham School Board: Purple line leaders won all four contested seats, flipping the board from conservative to progressive control. Outcomes included teacher pay raises, expanded mental health services, and new equity policies.

Carolina Federation: 403 purple line leaders | 9,581 total participants (2024)

Purple line leaders convert organizational capacity into electoral and policy outcomes.

Jason Dunkin (Rural Civic Alliance NC) organized residents to restore a local playground, helped elect the first Black Sheriff in his county, and contributed to flipping a state House seat by 228 votes—breaking the GOP supermajority.

Rural Civic Alliance NC: 592 purple line leaders | 16% retention rate (highest in sample)

Proposed Mechanisms (I)

  1. 1

    Relational bandwidth

    Purple line leaders maintain ongoing relationships with constituents, officials, and each other—enabling rapid coordination

  2. 2

    Credible commitment

    Elected officials respond differently to organizations they know will be there next cycle

  3. 3

    Local knowledge

    Embedded leaders know where the votes are, who is persuadable, and what issues resonate

Proposed Mechanisms (II)

  1. 4

    Mobilization reserve

    Purple line leaders can activate broader networks when needed—the base beneath the base

  2. 5

    Redundancy

    Multiple leaders across issues and geographies create resilience when any one is targeted or exits

  3. 6

    Culture of agency ★

    Sustained participation builds belief that collective action works—a self-reinforcing cycle

Three Theoretical Conversations

Mass Mobilization & Thresholds

Core Insight

Participation scale associated with regime-level disruption (the 3.5% rule) (Chenoweth & Stephan 2011)

Empirical Domain

Maximalist campaigns, autocratic overthrow

Purple Line Extension

Incremental policy influence at state and local level

Organizing vs. Mobilizing

Core Insight

Depth matters more than breadth (Han; McAlevey; Ganz 2025)

Empirical Domain

Logic and practice of civic depth

Purple Line Extension

Validated thresholds from longitudinal relational data

Exit, Voice, and Loyalty

Core Insight

Exit is default; voice requires investment (Hirschman 1970)

Empirical Domain

Individual disposition toward voice or exit

Purple Line Extension

Without leadership density, exit is structurally unavoidable

Limitations

  • Organizational self-selection into data use agreements
  • Measurement consistency across 38 organizations cannot be guaranteed
  • Observational design reduces but cannot eliminate confounding
  • Generalizability to unions, faith communities, and other forms remains open
21

Future Directions

  • What builds the purple line?
    • Companion paper (McKenna, Ganz, Lin) shows organizing workshops produce measurable shifts in collective agency
  • Why are thresholds where they are? Relational bandwidth hypothesis requires direct testing
  • Threshold variation by issue domain, organizational model, political context
  • Democratic backsliding: Does the redundancy mechanism become primary under authoritarian pressure?
