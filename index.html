14

This study introduces leadership density theory and a new empirical frontier for movement research

  • Leadership density theory: The participation-power gap is more likely a function of organizational leader density than mobilization intensity
  • New empirical frontier: Large-N participation microdata complements protest event datasets with leadership growth, retention, and team structure metrics
  • Actionable thresholds: 200-700 purple line leaders for statewide influence; 80-100 for local wins
  • Democratic backsliding: Muscular civic associations, not episodic mobilization, may be required to restore responsive governance
15

Conclusions

  • Scaled participation alone does not generate civic power
  • Structured teams of highly committed leaders convert mobilization into governing influence
  • The thresholds for local and state influence are achievable—hundreds, not thousands
  • State-based organizations with leadership density are the bulwark against authoritarian encroachment
